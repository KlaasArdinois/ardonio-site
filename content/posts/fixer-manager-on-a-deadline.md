Here's a number worth sitting with.

In [Lenny's 2026 survey of how tech workers are feeling](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/how-tech-workers-are-feeling-in-2026), manager effectiveness came out as the strongest driver of burnout in the entire dataset — beating role, company size, and even AI anxiety. Workers with a highly effective manager reported roughly 65% higher job enjoyment and dramatically lower burnout than those without one.

And yet only about a quarter of tech workers rate their manager as highly effective. More than a third rate theirs as outright ineffective. Those numbers have barely moved in a year.

Read that twice: **the most powerful retention lever in tech is also the one almost nobody is pulling.** That gap — most important, most neglected — is the reason an entire category of work exists. But before we get to that, a harder question hides inside the survey: effective at *what*?

# What an effective manager actually is

In my book, an effective manager is a combination of two things that don't always live in the same person.

The first is **human skill**: the ability to motivate, to relate, to be compassionate, and — crucially — to articulate the reasoning behind a decision. People will follow a call they disagree with if they understand *why* it was made.

The second is **earned opinion**: being decisive enough, and mature enough, to make a call and then stick with it. This is where "nice" managers often fall down, because they mistake niceness for agreement. It isn't. A good manager can be warm and still tell you no. "Nice" does not mean "agree at all times."

And with engineers specifically, there's a third property that matters more than most people expect: **consistency.** Engineers judge you far less on any single decision and far more on whether you're the same person from one week to the next — or whether the ground is constantly shifting under them. That's not a personality quirk, it's occupational. Their entire working world runs on deterministic, consistent systems: the same input produces the same output, every time. A manager whose decisions are unpredictable feels, to an engineer, like a flaky dependency — and they will quietly start routing around you. Consistency isn't rigidity. It's being someone people can model.

Hold those together — warmth, decisiveness, consistency — and you have the thing the survey is measuring but never defines. Now, back to the gap.

# The most neglected lever, and why a crisis can't wait

The number never budges because good management is slow to grow and easy to defer. You promote your best engineer, hand them a team, give them no support, and call it a management layer. In steady state you can just about get away with developing your way there over years.

In a crisis, you can't. A departed CTO, a demoralised team, a runway that's ticking — that's precisely the window where the manager gap does the most damage and takes the longest to close on its own. Which is the whole reason a fixer exists: **a fixer is a good manager on a deadline.**

# What the job actually looked like

Let me make it concrete.

The CTO had just left — founder and co-founder, gone. What he left behind wasn't a technical problem. The code still compiled, the servers still ran. What he left behind was a team that had lost its rudder. And the reflexive move — "find a new CTO, fast" — is a trap, because you cannot hire a great CTO in a panic, and the team cannot sit rudderless for the months a proper search takes.

So I came in to hold the middle, and the work sorted into three things. Notably, only one was technical.

**Stabilise the humans first.** This is not all cold, hard business. A team that's just lost its leader is anxious, and anxious people do their safest work, or they leave. Before anything else you steady them: be visible, be honest about what you know and don't, and give them a reason to believe the ship still has a course. Get this wrong and every other fix is moot — because the people who understand the system walk out the door.

**Build a baseline of process.** Not bureaucracy — structure. A rudderless team isn't suffering from too few rules, it's suffering from no shared sense of how decisions get made. A lightweight, dependable cadence gives people something solid to stand on while the bigger questions get answered. Structure, for humans, is a form of reassurance.

**Help the founder see the shape of the next CTO.** This is the one that sets up the exit. The founder didn't need me to *be* the next CTO — they needed help understanding what the next CTO should *look like* for the company they were becoming, not the one they'd been. That profile is the most valuable thing I left behind. _(this is the same idea as [The 4 Stages of a CTO](../4-stages-of-a-cto/) — the leader a company needs changes with its phase, and hiring for the last phase is how you end up back in this room in two years.)_

# The exit is the point, and you plan it on day one

Three months later, I was out. The founder had a clear plan: the VP Eng could hold the fort while they raised their next round and ran a proper search for the CTO the company actually needed.

That clean exit wasn't luck. It was the design.

A fixer is not an evergreen role. The engagement has an end built into it from the start — sometimes three months, sometimes eighteen, but always an end. And a good engagement *opens* by defining that exit: what does "stable enough to hand over" look like, who takes the wheel, and what has to be true for them to succeed? You plan backwards from the handover, because the handover is the actual product. Everything else is just the work required to make it clean.

The failure mode here has a name: the fixer who overstays. The intensity that stabilises a frightened team turns corrosive once the team is calm — you become the person manufacturing urgency nobody needs. A fixer who doesn't build their own replacement hasn't fixed anything; they've just made themselves load-bearing, which is the exact dependency trap you were brought in to remove. Knowing when to leave is part of the craft, not an afterthought to it.

# Same craft, different weather

It's tempting to hear all this and conclude that fixers and steady-state leaders are two different tiers of manager. They're not. A fixer has to clear exactly the same bar I set out earlier — warmth, earned opinion, consistency — and if anything has to clear it under *more* pressure, because a team in crisis needs effective management more, not less. A fixer who isn't a genuinely good manager isn't a fixer; they're an arsonist with a job title. This is not BAU-manager versus fixer, as if one were the lesser version. It's the same craft.

The difference is the situation in which each one thrives as a character.

Fixers are, frankly, a bit allergic to business-as-usual. Once the fire is out and the days get predictable, they get bored — and a bored fixer starts looking for fires, which is the last thing a newly-calm team needs. The people who are brilliant at the slow, patient, compounding work of building a team over years feel the opposite: drop them into the chaos of a leaderless team with a ticking runway and they're overwhelmed, because that's not the weather they do their best work in.

Neither is the better manager. They're the same skill in different conditions. The mistake is expecting one person to thrive in both — keeping the fixer around after the crisis because they did well, or parachuting a steady-state builder into a crisis because they're excellent in calm water. Matching the character to the situation is the entire game.

Which is why the best thing a fixer can do is make themselves redundant, on purpose, on a schedule. You come in when the team has lost its rudder, you hold the middle while the humans steady and the structure sets and the founder works out who they need next — and then you hand over the wheel and go. The measure of the job isn't what you fixed while you were there. It's whether it stays fixed after you've gone.
