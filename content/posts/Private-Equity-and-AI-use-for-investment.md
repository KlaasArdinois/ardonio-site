Outline
* Intro - Tension between single instance an aggregate data.
** Good investment outcomes are specific / idiosyncratic.
** Aggregation vs specifics 
-> The closer a task gets to validating assumptions against messy reality, the weaker generalized models become


# Opening hook

* PE lives on single deals, not deals in the aggregate
* Every fund's returns rest on a handful of specific decisions, not a portfolio of averages
* That single fact has more implications for how you should use AI than any technology review

# What PE actually underwrites

* Not just financial structure — a transformation thesis
* The financial model is table stakes. The judgment is the edge.
* That judgment draws on: operator experience, industry depth, deal pattern matching, reading incomplete and contradictory operational reality
* Brief note: this post is about fund operations specifically — for AI inside portfolio companies, link to JV piece

# The structural mismatch

* LLMs are aggregate systems — aggregate priors, aggregate language, aggregate patterns, aggregate plausibility
* Investment outcomes are driven by specifics — specific people, specific timing, specific operational realities, specific asymmetries
* These are not reconcilable by using a better prompt

# The upstream trap

* Surface area expanded ≠ conviction improved
* The danger: LLMs sound equally credible whether they're right or wrong. That's not a bug, it's how the models work.
* More plausible-sounding inputs can dilute judgment rather than sharpen it
* The trap is mistaking fluency for signal

# Why judgment is irreducibly human

* Judgment in PE isn't summarising known facts — it's interpreting complex, incomplete, contradictory operational reality
* The edge lives in the gap between what's documented and what's true
* An LLM cannot access that gap. It can only work with what's already been written down.
* Operator experience, pattern matching from prior deals, reading a management team — none of that is in the training data

# The constraint framing

* Theory of constraints: judgment is the scarce step. Everything else is throughput.
* The right question isn't "where can we use AI" — it's "how do we optimise around the constraint"
* That reframe changes the deployment logic entirely

# Where AI creates genuine ROI
 
* Decision hygiene: Feed the IC memo to an LLM and ask it to pressure-test. Catches the obvious holes before the IC room does. Distinction: AI attacks the memo, humans write it.
* Broader perspective upstream: Historical precedents, sector patterns, screening the universe — expanding the relevant input set. Useful as long as you remember it's aggregate, not specific.
* Analytical capacity: Modelling assistance, data synthesis, presentation checking. Frees analyst time and redirects it toward the constrained step — judgment.
* In each case: AI improves the conditions under which judgment is exercised. It doesn't substitute for it.

# The practical division

* Upstream of judgment: AI generates plausible assumptions, maps the possibility space. Input, not answer. Apply human filter for operational relevance.
* Judgment itself: human. This is where the returns are.
* Downstream of judgment: AI earns clear ROI — memo hygiene, model mechanics, data presentation.

# Closing

* The funds that will use AI well aren't the ones that use it most — they're the ones that deploy it precisely
* Optimise around the constraint. Protect the judgment. Use AI to improve the conditions around it.
* No cliche. Land on the constraint logic as the actionable takeaway.
